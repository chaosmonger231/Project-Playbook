1. What this playbook is for

An incident response plan is a written guide that helps an organization prepare for, respond to, and recover from cybersecurity incidents. A good plan reduces confusion, improves communication, and helps teams act faster when something goes wrong.

This page is meant to help your organization think through the structure of a response plan before you begin filling out the main incident response workspace.

This planning page is a practical starting point. Your organization may still need legal, technical, regulatory, or leadership review before relying on a final incident response plan.

2. Why incident response planning matters

  • It helps staff know what to do during a stressful situation.
  • It clarifies who makes decisions and who should be contacted first.
  • It helps preserve evidence and reduce accidental mistakes.
  • It improves coordination across technical staff, leadership, and communications roles.
  • It supports faster containment, recovery, and post-incident review.

3. Who should be involved

Incident response planning should not belong to only one person. Even small organizations should identify the people who may need to participate when an incident happens.

Common participants
  • Incident lead – coordinates the response process
  • Technical lead / IT support – investigates, contains, and restores systems
  • Leadership – approves major decisions and external actions
  • Communications contact – handles messaging to staff, users, or the public
  • Outside support – vendors, legal counsel, cyber insurance, or law enforcement if needed

4. Core parts of an incident response plan

A. Emergency Contacts

Your plan should list the people or groups who must be contacted first. Include names, roles, phone numbers, email addresses, and any notes that would help someone act quickly.

B. Quick Actions

Quick actions are the first steps your organization expects someone to take when a possible incident is discovered. These should be short, practical, and easy to follow under pressure.

C. Reporting and Escalation

Your organization should define who must be informed, when escalation is required, and whether any outside reporting obligations may apply. This helps avoid delays and confusion during an incident.

D. Response Lifecycle

A structured plan usually includes stages such as preparation, detection, containment, recovery, and review. This helps teams stay organized and avoid skipping important steps.

5. Simple before, during, and after model

Before an incident
  • Identify important systems, data, and services.
  • Assign roles and decision-makers.
  • Prepare contact lists and escalation paths.
  • Review backup readiness and recovery options.
  • Make sure staff know how to report suspicious activity.
During an incident
  • Confirm whether the event is credible.
  • Document what happened and when it was detected.
  • Preserve logs, screenshots, emails, or other evidence.
  • Contain the issue safely without creating more damage.
  • Communicate status updates to the right people.
After an incident
  • Recover systems and validate they are safe to use.
  • Review what worked and what failed.
  • Document lessons learned.
  • Improve policies, training, tooling, or communications based on the event.

6. Questions your organization should answer

  1. Who is the first person staff should contact if something suspicious happens?
  2. Who can approve containment steps, shutdowns, or major communications?
  3. Which systems or services are most critical to restore first?
  4. What evidence should be preserved during an incident?
  5. What outside contacts might be needed, such as a vendor or insurer?
  6. How will your organization decide when an event becomes an incident?
  7. How will your organization review and improve the plan afterward?
