Incident Response & Operations

Detection & Response Playbook

This playbook provides a practical starting point for identifying, analyzing, containing, and recovering from cybersecurity incidents. It is designed for small and mid-sized organizations that need a simple, structured workflow for handling alerts and suspicious activity.

Preparation Detection & Analysis Containment Recovery Lessons learned
This page follows a simplified 5-phase incident response lifecycle adapted from NIST guidance. It is meant to help organizations move from alert to action in a clear and repeatable way.

Why this playbook matters

Cyber incidents often begin as scattered warning signs: a suspicious login, an antivirus alert, an unusual process, a strange outbound connection, or unexpected file changes. Without a simple process, teams may delay action, miss escalation points, or lose valuable evidence.

A detection and response playbook helps define what to do first, who should be involved, how to assess severity, and how to move from detection into containment and recovery.

A simple way to think about this page: it is the general incident workflow. More specialized playbooks, such as ransomware response, can then build on top of this foundation.

The 5 Phases of Incident Response

This version breaks the response process into five practical phases that are easier to follow during a real event.

1. Preparation

Preparation is the work done before an incident occurs. The goal is to make sure your organization is ready to recognize and handle suspicious activity.

  • Ensure logging is enabled on important systems and services.
  • Put monitoring tools in place where possible.
  • Define staff roles, emergency contacts, and response responsibilities.
  • Create a clear escalation path for after-hours or urgent incidents.
  • Make sure critical systems, backups, and documentation are known in advance.
2. Detection & Analysis

Detection begins when an alert, report, or suspicious observation comes in. Analysis determines whether the activity is benign, suspicious, or malicious.

  • Review the alert source and what triggered it.
  • Identify affected hosts, users, processes, files, IP addresses, or services.
  • Determine whether the activity appears benign, suspicious, or malicious.
  • Estimate severity, scope, and urgency.
  • Decide whether the event should be escalated into a formal incident.
3. Containment

Containment aims to reduce damage and stop the incident from spreading while preserving enough information to continue investigation.

  • Isolate affected hosts if needed.
  • Block suspicious IP addresses, domains, or accounts if appropriate.
  • Disable compromised credentials when misuse is suspected.
  • Preserve logs, screenshots, alerts, and other evidence before making major changes.
  • Coordinate internally so actions are not duplicated or missed.
4. Eradication & Recovery

Once the threat is contained, focus on removing the cause, fixing weaknesses, and safely returning systems to normal operation.

  • Remove malicious files, scripts, persistence mechanisms, or processes.
  • Patch vulnerable software or correct the root cause.
  • Reset passwords or rotate credentials if necessary.
  • Restore from backups when required and verify integrity before reconnecting systems.
  • Monitor closely for recurrence after recovery steps are complete.
5. Post-Incident Activity

After the immediate event is over, capture lessons learned so the organization improves over time.

  • Document what happened and when.
  • Record actions taken, decisions made, and what evidence was collected.
  • Identify what worked well and what slowed the response down.
  • Update detections, procedures, contact lists, and training as needed.
  • Use the incident to strengthen future readiness.

Simple alert triage questions

When an alert comes in, a small organization does not need a complicated process. Start by answering a few basic questions:

Initial questions
  • What happened?
  • When did it happen?
  • Which host, user, or account is involved?
  • What tool or person reported it?
  • Is it still ongoing?
Decision questions
  • Is this benign, suspicious, or clearly malicious?
  • Could the activity spread or cause business disruption?
  • Should the device or account be isolated now?
  • Do leadership or outside partners need to be informed?
  • What evidence should be preserved before changes are made?
